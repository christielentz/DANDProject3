Introduction¶

In this report, we will look at a dataset containing a list of Brazilian medical patients; of specific interest is whether or not they were a no-show to their medical appointments. The data contains demographic information and health information about each patient, as well as information about the scheduling of the appointment. The goal of this project, then, is to look at several of these variables and to try to make some conclusions about what contributes to the likelihood that a person does now show up to their appointment.

Here, we will look at three questions:

1) What is the relationship between gender and likelihood of a no-show?
2) What is the relationship between age and likelihood of a no-show?
3) How does the likelihood of a no-show vary with how far in advance an appointment is made? Is this affected by a patient receiving a text reminder?

For each question, we have the same dependent variable, which is the likelihood of a no-show. We aim to see what effect the independent variables of gender, age, appointment scheduling, and text reminders have on whether or not a person shows up.

In [20]:
#reate a function to add the value counts to the dataframe
#https://stackoverflow.com/questions/35809098/how-to-merge-pandas-value-counts-to-dataframe-or-use-it-to-subset-a-dataframe
def add_counts(df, column):
    """Counts the number of each unique value in a given column of a dataframe, and then appends the data to the dataframe
        as an additional column
    
        df -- dataframe input, to which the column of counted values will be added
        column -- the column in the input dataframe which will be counted
    """
    
    #Create a name for tne new column
    count_col = column.lower() + '_count'
    
    #Create a new dataframe containing the value counts, and name the columns
    df_count = pd.DataFrame(df[column].value_counts().reset_index())
    df_count.columns = [column, count_col]
    
    #Merge the new column with the input column
    return pd.merge(df, df_count, how='inner', on=column)

Conclusions¶

Question #1: What is the relationship between gender and likelihood of a no-show?

Looking at the bar graph in part 1 of our analysis, which considers the proportion of patients of each gender who have a no-show appointment, we see that there is very little difference between male and female patients, with a slightly higher proportion of females having one. Based on this, it is most likely that there is not a relationship between the two; however, no tests have been done here to determine whether the difference between the two is statistically significant.

Question #2: What is the relationship between age and likelihood of a no-show?

Looking at the final plot in the question 2 analysis, we can see a few things. The likelihood of a no-show increases greatly from about age 0 to age 20; thus, it appears that throughout childhood, one is increasingly more likely to miss a scheduled appointment. Once we reach ~age 20, we see a gradual decrease in the proportion of patients who have had a no-show, leveling out as we approach age 60. So for adulthood, we see that as people get older, they are less likely to miss appointments.

This data though, is limited, and we cannot draw any definitive conclusions as to why we see the increase throughout childhood, followed by a decrease throughout adulthood. Further work would be to try to tie this data to other variables.

Question #3: How does the likelihood of a no-show vary with how far in advance an appointment is made? Is this affected by a patient receiving a text reminder?

The first thing that we see in this data makes perfect sense - a very low rate of no-shows when the appointment is scheduled within the current day. This is exactly what we would expect; because of the short time frame, the patients are less likely to forget or have circumstances come up which prevent them from attending their appointment.

After this, we see a gradual increase in the proportion of people who were a no-show, which then starts to fall off again as we get to large date differences. This again makes sense; up to a certain point, making the appointment in advance is good planning, but after a point it may become difficult to remember.

We also see that an SMS text reminder has a slightly beneficial effect, with nearly all ages showing a decreased proportion of no-shows when a reminder was received.
